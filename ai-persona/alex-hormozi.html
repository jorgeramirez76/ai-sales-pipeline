What the Hormozi methodology emphasizes

Alex Hormozi's core teaching in $100M Offers is the Value Equation: Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort). To make an offer irresistible, you maximize the numerator (what the prospect gets + how certain they are of getting it) while minimizing the denominator (how long they wait + how much work they have to do). Layered on top is the Grand Slam Offer: an offer so good the prospect feels stupid saying no — combining the core deliverable, premium bonuses, guarantees that reverse risk, scarcity (limited slots), and urgency (deadline to decide). Finally, Hormozi's volume-negotiation principle: the person who can take more 'no's wins. Pipeline depth matters more than close rate.

Objection handling the Hormozi way

Hormozi's framework for objections: the only three real objections are 'I don't believe you,' 'I don't have the money,' 'I'm not sure now.' Every other objection is a surface disguise. The AI is trained to identify which of the three the prospect is actually expressing, and handle it with the corresponding Hormozi move: for 'I don't believe you,' proof stacking (specific case studies, reviews, results); for 'I don't have money,' value-reframe + payment flexibility; for 'I'm not sure now,' urgency + scarcity re-emphasis without being cheesy about it.

What if my business isn't a good fit for Grand Slam Offers?

Most businesses are — but not all. Grand Slam Offers work best when (1) your unit economics support adding bonuses, (2) you have the operational capacity to honor guarantees, (3) your buyers actually care about the dimensions Hormozi optimizes. Impulse purchases under $50 don't need a Grand Slam Offer. Complex B2B deals over $100K do, but the execution is different.

How is this different from a regular sales AI?

A generic sales AI runs whatever script you feed it. A Hormozi-trained AI actively builds the offer live in the conversation based on what the prospect reveals — maximizing Dream Outcome and minimizing Time Delay for that specific person. It's the difference between reading a script and applying a framework.

Does this work for services, or only products?

Both. Hormozi's examples in $100M Offers are mostly services (Gym Launch, consulting, software). The Value Equation maps equally well to services — in fact, services often have the biggest Time Delay and Effort components to minimize, which is where Grand Slam bonuses (concierge onboarding, done-for-you setup) create the most perceived value.
